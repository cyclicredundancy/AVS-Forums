Actually you can express points and lines at infinity using homogenous coordinates. You add a scalar value to your existing coordinates, so (x,y,w) for 2D and (x,y,z,w) for 3D.

To map homogenous coordinates to plain ones, use (x/w,y/w) for 2D and (x/w,y/w,z/w) for 3D. The reverse mapping is just (x,y,1) and (x,y,z,1). The points (x,y,z,w) and (2x,2y,2z,2w) represent the same point in 3D.

By setting w to 0, you get a point at infinity.

If you take two parallel lines in 2D:
x-y=1
x-y=2

then you convert them to homogenic coords by replacing the constant term with a w term:
x-y=w
x-y=2*w
which solves to:
0=w (subtract second equation from first)
x-y=0 (first equation updated)
x=y

so any point of form (a,a,0) is a solution, which represents the point at infinity at 45°, which is where these two parallel lines 'intersect'.

Using homogenous coordinates in 3D allows you to express any 3D operation (translation, rotation, shearing, perspective projection, parallel projection, ...) as a 4D matrix. Without homogenous coords, you can't do translations and projections.

In fact, it allows you to separate points (non-zero scalar) from vectors (zero scalar). If you add a vector to a point, you get a point again. Adding two points is not allowed, but you can add their difference (vector) to another point.
